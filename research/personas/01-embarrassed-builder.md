# Persona 1 — The Embarrassed Builder

> **One-liner:** A technical founder who shipped a working product that *looks* like developers made it — and knows the ugly UI is now costing them users and credibility, but can't fix it themselves and dreads managing a "creative."

*Evidence base: strong (raw HN founder quotes + Reddit). This is the warmest lead and the hardest to manage.*

---

## Stage & context
- **Funding/stage:** Pre-seed to Seed. Often pre-revenue or just past first users.
- **Team:** 1–5. Founder is technical (or a technical co-founder pair). Built the MVP themselves, increasingly with AI tooling (v0, Cursor, Lovable) plus their own code.
- **What they've built:** A functional product. The logic works. The interface is "average at best" — inconsistent, dated, untrustworthy.
- **On fire right now:** Users bounce or don't activate; an investor or a user said it "looks unprofessional"; they're about to put it in front of people who matter and the UI is an embarrassment. They *know* design is the missing leg and they have neither the skill nor the time.

## Jobs to be done
- **Functional:** Make the product look credible and usable *without slowing engineering down*. Translate their logic into flows users trust. Hand off design that drops cleanly into their stack (React/Next/Tailwind) — not a Figma file someone has to reverse-engineer.
- **Emotional:** Stop being embarrassed to demo it. Not have to babysit a designer whose process feels alien to a logical brain. Feel like an *adult is handling design* so they can stay on the product.

## The hiring trigger
The "**code is done, design is bad**" moment. Concretely: a working build + external signal that it looks amateur (investor feedback, a churn spike, a competitor that looks far more polished), and the realization that they cannot design their way out of it themselves.

## "Long-term" in their head
- **What it looks like:** A senior designer who already knows the product and can be pinged in a crunch with **low latency** — "knows you and can work fast when there's a crunch… removes the latency of one-off task platforms." Not a project; an on-call partner who absorbs context once and reuses it.
- **Commits when:** the person *self-directs and pushes back* — proposes the right thing instead of waiting to be told; speaks dev so handoff is frictionless; reduces (not adds to) their management load.
- **Churns when:** they have to art-direct ("tell them where to place things"), the designer needs hand-holding, the process is slow/heavy, or work can't be built without rework.

## Scar tissue → objections
- Hired "Figma jockeys" who delivered **pretty-but-unbuildable** mockups; great screenshots, no UX, no shippable code.
- Designers who "**very rarely propose major improvements**" and needed everything spelled out.
- The friction of managing "someone whose creative process is completely different from your logical approach."
- → **Objection:** "Another designer is just more management overhead and a Figma file my engineers can't use."

## Decision criteria & hard deal-breakers
- **Wants:** judgment + autonomy ("can mostly direct themselves"); front-end-aware handoff; speed; gets "done, not perfect"; modern-tooling-fluent.
- **Deal-breakers:** needs too much direction; long discovery before any design; aesthetics over function; can't hand off to dev cleanly; indifference to AI/prototyping tooling; "process theater."

## Where they look / how they vet
- **Where:** Hacker News, founder Slacks/Discords, referrals; Dribbble/Behance for discovery but *distrusts* them ("Figma jockeys"). The "designer with a GitHub *and* a Dribbble" = the unicorn they're hunting.
- **Vets by:** a small **paid trial** ("not more than a week"), a **real Figma walkthrough** (not polished shots), and **UX questions** to separate "design thinkers from Figma jockeys."

## Messaging that LANDS vs that REPELS
- **Lands:** "I design with what ships in mind — drops straight into your React/Next/Tailwind." · "Senior judgment, not a pixel-pusher: I propose the right call, you don't art-direct me." · "I speak both design and dev, so there's no rework tax." · "Fast, opinionated, low-latency once I know your product."
- **Repels:** discovery decks, "my creative process," brand-workshop language, slow timelines, anything that reads as *more* management for them. Don't sell "beautiful" — sell *handled, buildable, fast*.
